Analyzing White-Tailed Deer Reproductive Parameters in Response to Sharpshooting as a Method to Control for Chronic Wasting Disease

-Damian Satterthwaite-Phillips

Skip ahead to data analysis

Introduction

Culling is a frequently used method for controlling disease in wildlife populations. In the case of density-dependent disease, culling has a two-fold effect—it reduces contact rates and therefore disease transmission between hosts (Barlow 1996), and by culling infective hosts, the infective contribution of that host is also diminished. Modeling tools indicate that culling may also be an effective strategy when the disease is frequency dependent (Potapov et al. 2012). State agencies rely on hunter harvest to maintain deer population structures, and mathematical models specific to chronic wasting disease (CWD) control emphasize the importance of population reduction (Gross and Miller 2001, Wasserberg et al. 2009). In Illinois, CWD is managed through a combination of hunter harvest and intensive localized sharpshooting in CWD-infected areas.

Chronic wasting disease in white-tailed deer (Odocoileus virginianus) was first reported in Illinois just south of the Wisconsin state line from a suspect deer in November 2002 (IDNR 2003). Statewide surveillance of hunter-harvested deer that year detected 6 additional cases in northern Illinois. Subsequent to detection of the first CWD case, the Illinois Department of Natural Resources (IDNR) initiated an annual sharpshooting (SS) program to decrease deer densities, remove CWD-infected deer, and manage the disease in localized areas of high risk of disease transmission and where CWD was known to occur (IDNR 2003). The sharpshooting (SS) program in Illinois was non-selective and was intended to:

  1. reduce deer populations to a degree greater than hunting in sections infected with CWD or at high risk of infection, and
  2. introduce a mechanism of disease control in areas with limited hunting grounds and high human density.

Because of the timing of breeding and fawning relative to conventional hunting seasons, reproduction in white-tailed deer (WTD) is often evaluated using observational estimates of fawn numbers (offspring per doe), lactation rates in does, or examination of ovarian corpora albicantia during harvest (Schwartz 1998). In contrast, SS took place after the breeding season, while does were pregnant, allowing us to take measurements on fetuses directly.

Fecundity in WTD, indicated by pregnancy rates (number of pregnant does / total number of does) and fetuses-per-pregnancy is influenced by a number of factors such as the age of the mother and habitat quality. In Ohio, with deer habitat similar to that in northern Illinois, WTD were estimated to average 2.0 fetuses per pregnancy (Nixon 1971). In other regions, fetuses per pregnancy have been reported to be somewhat lower: 1.5 in Texas (Kie and White 1985); 1.6 in Iowa (Haugen 1975); 1.6 – 1.7 in South Carolina (Dapson et al. 1979). The relatively high numbers of fetuses per pregnancy in Ohio indicate the high-quality habitat for WTD, a condition similar to that of Illinois. Fawn pregnancy rates have been reported to range from 2-29% (DeYoung 2011). In a captive deer population in Michigan with supplemental food, as many as half the doe fawns became pregnant (Ozoga 1987). The Michigan study further indicates that high doe fawn fecundity and the frequency of triplet and quadruplet litters in older does reflect high-quality habitat and excellent nutrition (Ozoga 1987).

Factors that affect fecundity may also affect fetal development as indicated by fetal crown-rump length, fetal mass or fetal sex ratios (Jacobson 1976). Sex ratios in WTD deer are sensitive to time of conception during estrus with later conception leading to higher proportions of males. Although time of conception may be density dependent (Verme & Ozoga 1981) Bonenfant et al. (2003) did not find significant changes in the sex ratio at any density below carrying capacity. However, in red deer (Cervus elaphus), the proportion of males born declined as population density (Kruuk et al. 1999) and nutritional stress (Mysterud et al. 2000) increased.

Because SS in Illinois has been conducted over 10 years during pregnancy, it allowed us to measure reproduction based on observed fetal characteristics directly; over the last 10 years we have collected data from 5594 fetuses taken from 4748 does. These numbers are an order of magnitude larger than frequently cited papers on deer reproduction which commonly range in the hundreds. The direct measures of reproduction examined in this study will provide information on the response of deer to CWD management intervention through very localized population reduction efforts. Our objectives were:

  1. to provide summary statistics of reproductive parameters in Illinois WTD
  2. to examine the impact of the Illinois SS program on reproduction and fetal development in white-tailed deer; and
  3. to assess whether CWD affected reproductive parameters.

Because the SS program differs from hunting in that higher rates of culling occur in smaller, more focused loci, we examined its impact on reproduction at the same geographic scale. Illinois provides high-quality deer habitat and deer densities have been reported to be below carrying capacity (Roseberry and Woolf 1991, 1998). Therefore, we predicted that an increase in SS intensity would not significantly affect:

  1. fecundity
  2. fetal sex ratios
  3. fetal development.
To our knowledge, this study represents the largest dataset of does and fetuses ever available to study reproductive characteristics in deer. The magnitude of these data allows for detection of relatively small changes and with high statistical significance.

Study Area

Our study area included portions of northern Illinois, USA, where SS was conducted for disease control within 13 counties affected by CWD between 2003 and 2010. The Public Land Survey System divides the state of Illinois into a grid of 9.7 km by 9.7 km squares, each a unique township. Because of state line boundaries, and occasionally other historical divisions, some townships differ in size, but the vast majority are 94.1 square km (36 sq. miles; US Dept. of the Interior 2009, 2011). Each township is further subdivided into a grid of 1.6 km by 1.6 km squares called sections. The section was our geographical unit of analysis. Locations of all deer culled by sharpshooting were recorded to the appropriate township, range, and section according to the Public Land Survey System (US Dept. of the Interior 2011).

Throughout the greater region of northern Illinois, the landscape has been described as highly fragmented. The deer habitat consists of patches of forest, mixed with both agricultural and urban regions with urban development greatest in the Chicago metropolitan area (Piccolo et al. 2001). The fragmented patches of woodland provide substantial amounts of preferred edge habitat (Whitehead 1972) found at the forest-field boundaries and along wooded riparian corridors (Nixon et al. 1991, 2008). The study area is part of the Great Plains region, with minimal topographical relief, and has temperate continental climate with cold winters (mean = –3° C in January) and warm summers (mean = 24° C in July; Nixon et al. 2008).

Methods

Sharpshooting for CWD control.—

IDNR conducts a statewide program for CWD monitoring based on the following sources of samples: suspect deer, hunter harvest, deer population control permits, deer reduction permits, deer-vehicle collisions and SS. The Illinois Department of Agriculture Animal Disease Laboratories in Galesburg and Centralia, (IL) used retropharyngeal lymph nodes and brain tissue (obex) for CWD testing using immunohistochemistry (O’Rourke et al. 1998) as the diagnostic test. Chronic wasting disease control relies on an adaptive management strategy based on SS to reduce the deer population in infected sections, and high-risk areas (including at least a 2-section buffer around each positive section; Shelton and Hulin 2004). The SS sites are thus limited to a square with 8-km sides. Sharpshooting took place from 15 January to 30 March each year, shortly after the completion of the breeding season, following the identification of CWD-infected sections from prior surveillance. Biologists from IDNR baited sites, and removed all deer that came to the site, without intention of selective removal by age or sex.

Biologists from IDNR determined sex and age of culled deer. Age was determined by dental morphology and wear (Severinghaus 1949). This aging method can overestimate the age of young adults (2 yrs), and underestimate age in older (≥4 yrs) deer (Hamlin et al. 2000, Gee et al. 2002, Meares et al. 2006). We therefore limited our classification to fawns (<1 yr), yearlings (1 - 2 yrs) and adults (≥2 yrs). Biologists from IDNR also collected and submitted fetus samples from the deer culled by SS to the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, where we recorded fetal crown-rump length (Jacobson 1976), sex, mass and litter size.

Study Design

We measured reproductive traits from all does killed by SS in a given year. These traits included the percentage of pregnant does, the number of fetuses per pregnancy, fetal development as measured by the ratio of crown-rump length to mass, and fetal sex ratios. The level of prior SS for each section was quantified by prior intensity, defined as the mean number of does culled annually from the section in all prior years. Intensity is calculated as a cumulative value over all preceding years excluding the present year. (Because SS occurred after the breeding season, it could not impact reproductive parameters until subsequent breeding seasons.) For instance, a prior intensity value of 7 indicates a mean of 7 deer culled per year by SS across all sharpshot years in that section prior to the current year’s measurements of reproduction. That is,

Prior intensityt

  • = sum(ni) / (t - 1); t > a, and
  • = 0; t = a

for i = (1, 2, ..., t - 1), where t is the year of the given sample, a is the first year in which the given section was sharpshot, and ni represents the number of deer culled in the given section in the ith year. The numerator represents the total number of deer culled prior to year t (t = the year in which reproductive measurements were taken), and the denominator is the total number of elapsed years in that period since SS was initiated. When t = a, this represents the first year that SS occurred in the section; because there was no prior SS, prior intensity = 0. Each data point represents the prior intensity per section per year. Because SS was a sustained as well as an adaptive CWD control and management strategy, different sections received different numbers of SS years so, for a given section, prior intensity may have changed over time as additional SS occurred. Prior intensity values ranged among sections from 0 to 46 females culled per year.
