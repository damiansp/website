Damian Satterthwaite-Phillips, PhD

Statistician, Linguist, Anthropologist, and Scientific Consultant

I have always despised the phrase "Jack of all trades, master of none". While I admire people who can spend a lifetime focused on a given area of expertise, the world needs generalists as well--people with a wide breadth of knowledge, and the ability to apply learning from one area analogously to others. The "Renaissance Man" ideal, basically, is what I strive for, however imperfectly. I have a great sense of wonder, curiosity, and interest in a good many things. Nevertheless, I do have a few areas that I have focused more of my efforts on, including statistics, linguistics, anthropology, and computer science. I am also especially interested in devoting more of my time to help in the preservation of endangered languages.

I received my doctorate in anthropology at Stanford University in 2011, where my research focused on developing statistical methods for inferring the historical relationships between languages. In the course of this research, I spent several years in southwest China, near the Burmese and Thailand borders, gathering data on several endangered languages.

After completing m doctorate, I went on to do postdoctoral research at the Illinois Natural History Survey (INHS)--a division of the Prairie Research Institute at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. During my time at INHS, I served primarily as a statistician and mathematical modeler. While furthering my statistical education, I applied these methods to research in wildlife management. I was involved in multiple research projects, including:

  • Research on toxins in Illinois watersheds
  • Developing methods for inferring predator diets from their fatty acid content. Another shorter summary available here (p. 4).
  • Research on the effects of culling diseased deer on population dynamics. Interim report here (p. 4-5) . Fuller academic report currently in preparation.
  • Research on the role of heavy metals in white-tailed deer susceptibility to chronic wasting disease .

After completion of my postdoctorate, I went on to be the lead scientist for a subsistence mapping project at the Northwest Arctic Borough in Alaska. The Borough is a native home-rule government, and the mapping project documents current land use by the local IÃ±upiaq ("Eskimo") population, documenting where hunting, trapping, fishing, and plant gathering occur. The mapping project is intended to serve as a tool to help development occur in a way that minimally impacts subsistence resources. I continue to collaborate with the Borough on this project. The atlas in expected to be published late 2015 or early 2016. (Samples available upon request.) Some of the statistical methods are available on the Borough's website.

I have also performed statistical analyses for social scientists. I collaborated with anthropologists conducting research on the role of women's labor and marriage mobility in China. See article here. I am currently working with one of the coauthors on a related publication to be submitted soon.
