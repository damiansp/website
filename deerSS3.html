Deer Reproduction & Disease Control:
Statistical Analyses (part 2)

Damian Satterthwaite-Phillips

Pregnancy Rates

For the next section, we want to know if pregnancy rates are being affected by the culling. We model pregnancy as the response to be predicted by sharpshooting intensity, along with other reasonable predictors in our data set as covariates. Because the 'pregnant' variable is either 1 or 0 (yes or no), the response is binary, and not normally distributed. In such a case, a generalized linear model with residuals modeled as binomially distributed. (In this case, where the response is binary, this is the same as logistic regression.)

First though, we examine the relationship between pairs of variables to alert us to potential non-linear relationships.

First though, to make things simple, reorder age, so that the levels are in ascending order of age.

levels(deerF1$age)

## [1] "adult"    "fawn"     "yearling"

deerF1$age = factor(deerF1$age, levels = c('fawn', 'yearling', 'adult'))
levels(deerF1$age)

## [1] "fawn"     "yearling" "adult"

Also, we want to use the year variable to correct for stochastic/random variation from one year to the next. (This might actually be a proxy for many other things like temperature, rainfall, food supply, etc. that we do not have in our data set, but that nevertheless cause annual variations.) Hence, it may be better to treat year as a categorical variable rather than a continous one, though we will compare both models.

deerF1$yearF = as.factor(deerF1$year)

Now on to comparing pairs of variables.

modVars = deerF1[, c('pregnant', 'intensity', 'age', 'year', 'section', 'cwd')]
pairs(modVars, panel = panel.smooth)

Intensity, age, and year all look like they may potentially have non-linear relationships with some of the other variables, so to potentially capture this in the model, we will include their squared terms as predictors as well.

This model is somewhat better by the AIC (3245.1). There is similarly a reduction in deviance, but at the expense of more parameters. The reduced model suggests that sharpshooting intensity (both linear and nonlinear terms) does have a significant affect on pregnancy. Additionally, there are signifcant differences in pregnancy by year, and the age of the mother (as we would expect), and there are significant differences by location as well. The overall inferences are not changed by the omission of the influential points. Sharpshooting intensity, year, location, and the age of the mother all all found to be significant predictors of pregnancy.

Using the last model, we will visualize the effects of intensity and age of the mother on pregnancy. We will look at these for the 'typical' year and location by using the year and section with the median coefficient in the model.

# median coef for year
yrCoef = median(c(0, coef(preg.glm.redi)[6:14]))
sectCoef = median(c(0, coef(preg.glm.redi)[16:312]))
yrlYrCoef = median(c(0, coef(preg.glm.redi)[seq(315, 331, 2)]))
adYrCoef = median(c(0, coef(preg.glm.redi)[seq(316, 332, 2)]))

In R, we can use the predict function to do the work for us here, but to make it explicit, we enter the equation manually. The model, from above reduced to just include the 'typical' section and year, for cwd-negative deer is:

preg = 0.1606 - 0.01351*int + 0.001177*int2 + 0.05933*yrl + 0.3318*ad + yrCoef + sectCoef - 0.00001948*int3 + yrlYrCoef*yrl + adYrCoef*ad

…where yrl and ad are dummy variables for yearling and adult respectively (i.e. = 1 if true, 0 if false). Also, because the canonical link for the binomial family of glms is the logit link (used by default if not specified in glm(…, family = binomial)), the predictors are given in logits. We will need to do the inverse function to return them to the original scale.

illogit = function(x) {
        exp(x) / (1+ exp(x))
}

pregPred = function(int, yrl, ad) {
    predLogit = 0.1606 - 0.01351*int + 0.001177*int^2 + 0.05933*yrl + 
        0.3318*ad + yrCoef + sectCoef - 0.00001948*int^3 + yrlYrCoef*yrl + 
        adYrCoef*ad
    return(illogit(predLogit))
}

So, what we see is, as expected fawns have far lower pregnancy rates than older deer, and adults, slightly more than yearlings. As intensity levels increase up to about 35, pregnancy rates are actually predicted to increase as well. One possible explanation is that sharpshooting reduces local deer density, and hence competition, providing greater access to nutritional resources. Note though, that the variation is actually quite small. For fawns, the predicted preganancy rates vary only from a minimum of 54.9% to a maximum of 59.3%, less than 5% variation over all levels of intensity.
