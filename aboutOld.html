Damian Satterthwaite-Phillips, PhD

Company Founder and Principal Consultant

Damian received his doctorate in anthropology at Stanford University in 2011, where his research focused on developing statistical methods for inferring the historical relationships between languages. In the course of this research, he spent several years in China gathering data on several endangered languages.

After completing his doctorate, Damian went on to do postdoctoral research at the Illinois Natural History Survey (INHS)--a division of the Prairie Research Institute at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. During his time at INHS, he served primarily as a statistician and mathematical modeler. While furthering his statistical education, he applied these methods to research in wildlife management. He was involved in multiple research projects, including:

  • Research on toxins in Illinois watersheds
  • Developing methods for inferring predator diets from their fatty acid content (in press with the Journal of Animal Science and Technology; link forthcoming). Brief Report (p. 4-5)
  • Research on the effects of culling diseased deer on population dynamics. Interim report here (p. 4-5). Fuller academic report currently in preparation.
  • Research on the role of heavy metals in white-tailed deer susceptibility to chronic wasting disease (journal article currently in peer review).

After completion of his postdoctorate, Damian went on to be the lead scientist for a subsistence mapping project at the Northwest Arctic Borough in Alaska. The mapping project is intended to serve as a tool to help development occur in a way that minimally impacts subsistence resources. Phillips Research & Analytics continues to collaborate with the Borough on this project. Damian has also performed statistical analyses for social scientists. He collaborated with anthropologist conducting research on the role of women's labor and marriage mobility in China. See article here. He is currently working with one of the coauthors on a similar publication to be submitted for publication soon.
